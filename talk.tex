\section{Application}

\subsection{Statics}
\begin{frame}{Variational Approach for Finding Ground States}

\begin{enumerate}
\pause
\item Objective:  Find the lowest eigenvalue of $\textbf{H}$, i.e. the lowest $E$ such that $\textbf{H}\cdot \vec{S} = E \vec{S}$ for some $\vec{S}.$
\pause
\item Equivalent to finding the state vector $\vec{S}$ to minimize the Rayleigh quotient $R(\vec{S}):=\frac{\vec{S}^*\cdot \textbf{H} \cdot \vec{S}}{\vec{S}^*\cdot \vec{S}}.$
\pause
\item Since we don't know $\vec{S}$ \emph{a priori}, make a guess with tunable parameters (an ``ansatz'') and vary these parameters to minimize $R(\vec{S}).$
\pause
\item The ``divide and conquer'' (i.e. matrix product state) approach gives us an excellent ansatz for this purpose.
\pause
\begin{enumerate}
\item $R(\vec{S})$ can be evaluated efficiently.
\pause
\item Parameters can be optimized efficiently.
\pause
\item It does a good job of approximating the true ground state.
\end{enumerate}
\end{enumerate}

\end{frame}

\begin{frame}{Summary}
    \begin{itemize}
        \item Divide and Conquer is a general strategy vital to understanding complex systems, but for systems with intrinsic non-determinism one must have a more nuanced sense of how to ``divide'' the system.
        \item Matrix product states provides one means of ``dividing'' up one-dimensional systems, resulting in effective methods for simulating them.
        \item Future work includes applying these ideas to two dimensional systems, and exploring connections these ideas turn out to have with formal language theory.
    \end{itemize}
\end{frame}
